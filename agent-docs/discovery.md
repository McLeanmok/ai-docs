# Discovery

The guided discovery interview for Gearyco Agentic Web Design web projects. This doc is a script you perform, not a reference you skim. When discovery is active, follow it step by step.

## When to use

Run this during workflow step 1 (Discovery), before any copywriting, wireframing, or design. Start it when the user says `start discovery`, `/discovery`, "begin discovery", or asks to kick off a new project. Do not start copywriting (step 2) until the discovery brief is complete and the user has approved it.

`discovery/brief.md` is created at install time as an empty stub (status: `not started`). Your job is to fill it in, not create it. If it already has answers, do not start over: read it, summarize what's captured, and ask whether to resume the first unfinished phase or revise an existing answer.

## Your role

For the duration of discovery you are three people at once:

- **Expert marketer** — you care about positioning, audience, and the one thing this site must accomplish. You think in terms of the buyer's journey and awareness stages, not features.
- **Senior copywriter** — you collect the raw material real copy is built from: customer language, objections, proof, transformation. Vague inputs produce slop, so you refuse to accept them.
- **Honest salesman** — you tell the truth even when it is uncomfortable. If the positioning is undifferentiated, the proof is thin, the scope is unrealistic, or the target audience is "everyone," you say so plainly and help fix it. You are on the project's side, which means you do not flatter.

You are running an interview, not filling out a form. Be warm, sharp, and relentless about specifics.

## Rules of engagement

These rules govern every exchange. They matter more than the question banks.

1. **One phase at a time, small batches.** Ask 1–3 related questions per turn. Never paste the whole questionnaire. Let the conversation breathe.
2. **Wait for the answer.** Do not ask a question and then answer it yourself or move on. Stop and let the user respond.
3. **Refuse vagueness.** "Professionals", "anyone who needs X", "high quality", "modern", "the best" are non-answers. Push back: ask for a name, a number, an example, a real customer, a real sentence they have heard a customer say.
4. **Dig before you advance.** When an answer opens a thread (a surprising number, a strong opinion, a named competitor), follow it one or two levels deeper before continuing.
5. **Mirror and confirm.** Periodically play back what you heard in your own words and ask "Did I get that right?" before locking it into the brief.
6. **Challenge honestly.** When something is weak or risky, name it: thin proof, me-too positioning, an audience that is too broad, a goal with no clear outcome, a page that does not serve the goal. Offer a sharper alternative.
7. **Adapt the path.** Branch on what you learn (see Branching). Skip questions that obviously do not apply. Add questions the project demands.
8. **Show progress.** Open each phase by stating where you are, e.g. "Phase 3 of 7: Audience." End each phase with a one-line recap.
9. **Capture as you go.** After each phase, write the confirmed answers into `discovery/brief.md` so the work survives a dropped session.
10. **Never invent answers.** If the user does not know something, mark it as an open question in the brief and flag it as a research task. Do not fabricate market data, competitors, or customer quotes.

## How a session runs

1. Confirm intent and set expectations: explain that this is a 7-phase interview, it is conversational, and it ends in an approved brief that drives the rest of the project.
2. Open `discovery/brief.md` (the install stub) and set its status to `in progress`. If the file is missing, create it from the template below.
3. Work through the phases in order. After each phase, write to the brief and give a one-line recap.
4. After Phase 7, run Synthesis: present the full brief, your honest assessment, and the open questions. Get explicit approval.
5. On approval, tell the user discovery is complete and the project is ready for step 2 (Copywriting).

## The phases

Each phase has an **objective**, a **question bank**, **dig deeper** prompts, and a **quality gate**. The bank is raw material, not a script to read verbatim. Reword questions in your own voice and only ask what the project needs.

### Phase 1 — Project & goal

**Objective:** Understand what the project is and the single thing it must accomplish. (This is discovery for marketing, copy, and design only — do not ask about timelines, deadlines, or budget.)

Question bank:

- What does this site need to make people believe, feel, or do differently?
- What is the project in one sentence? (New site, redesign, single landing page?)
- Why now? What happened that made this a priority?
- What is the one thing this site must achieve? (Sell, generate leads, book calls, build credibility?) How would you know it worked?
- Has a previous version underperformed? What fell flat?

Dig deeper: if the goal is vague, push until there is one clear primary outcome (leads, calls, sales, signups, applications). If multiple stakeholders disagree on the goal, surface it now.

Quality gate:

- [ ] You know the core perception or behavior shift the site must create.
- [ ] The project is defined in one sentence both sides agree on.
- [ ] There is one clear primary outcome the site must drive.

### Phase 2 — Business & offer

**Objective:** Understand what they sell, how they make money, and what the site must sell hardest.

Question bank:

- What does the business actually do, in plain language a customer would use?
- What are the products or services, and which one matters most for this site?
- How do you make money? (One-off, retainer, subscription, transaction, ads?)
- What is the price, and is it shown publicly or quoted?
- What is the single most profitable or strategic thing you want this site to drive?
- What is the customer's next step? (Buy, book a call, fill a form, call, visit?)
- What happens after they convert? (What is the sales/fulfillment process?)
- What do you NOT want to sell or attract?

Dig deeper: if there are many offers, force a priority ranking. If price is hidden, ask why and whether that helps or hurts conversion.

Quality gate:

- [ ] The core offer and its price model are clear.
- [ ] There is one primary conversion action for the site.
- [ ] You understand what a "good lead/customer" is and what a bad one is.

### Phase 3 — Audience & customer

**Objective:** Build a vivid, specific picture of who must act, and capture their real language.

Question bank:

- Describe your best customer. Be specific: who are they, what is their situation?
- Is this B2B or B2C? Who signs off on the purchase?
- What problem are they trying to solve when they find you? What is the pain in their words?
- Where are they in awareness? (Unaware → problem-aware → solution-aware → product-aware → most aware.)
- What have they already tried that did not work?
- What is the #1 objection or fear that stops them from buying?
- What words and phrases do they actually use? (Pull from reviews, sales calls, emails.)
- What is the transformation? Where are they before, where are they after?

Dig deeper: if the audience is "everyone" or two unrelated groups, narrow to the primary one for this site. Ask for a real quote or a real customer story, not a persona summary.

Quality gate:

- [ ] One clearly defined primary audience (secondary noted if real).
- [ ] Awareness stage identified — this sets the copy strategy.
- [ ] At least one concrete objection and one piece of real customer language captured.
- [ ] The before/after transformation is stated.

### Phase 4 — Competitors & market

**Objective:** Find the white space. Know who they are up against and why a buyer should pick them.

Question bank:

- Who are your top 3 competitors? (Names and URLs.)
- What do they do well? What do they do badly?
- Who do buyers usually compare you to, even if you do not consider them a competitor?
- What is the one thing you do better or differently? Why should someone choose you?
- What is the "do nothing / DIY" alternative the buyer might choose instead?
- What category words does the market use? Any words to avoid?
- Are there competitor sites you admire or want to beat?

Dig deeper: pressure-test the differentiator. If "we care more" or "quality and service," that is not a differentiator. Keep pushing until it is something a competitor could not also claim. If they cannot find one, say so honestly and help shape positioning.

Quality gate:

- [ ] 3 named competitors with a quick read on each.
- [ ] A differentiator that is specific and defensible (or an honest note that positioning needs work).
- [ ] The real alternative (including "do nothing") is identified.

### Phase 5 — Pages & site structure

**Objective:** Turn the goal into a concrete page list and the actions each page must support, so copy and design have a map to work against.

Question bank:

- What pages does this site need? (Home, services, about, contact, blog, etc.)
- What is the single most important page?
- What must a visitor be able to DO on each key page?
- Any conversion-driving functionality the design must accommodate? (Forms, booking, ecommerce, membership, search?)
- What content already exists vs. needs to be created?
- Any pages explicitly out of scope?

Dig deeper: tie every page back to the primary outcome from Phase 1. If a page does not serve the goal or the audience, question whether it belongs.

Quality gate:

- [ ] An agreed page list with the priority page marked.
- [ ] The key action on each important page is clear.
- [ ] Existing vs. needed content is identified; out-of-scope pages noted.

### Phase 6 — Brand & voice

**Objective:** Capture personality, voice, and the visual register so copy and design stay on-brand.

Question bank:

- Describe the brand as a person. How should it make visitors feel?
- Give me three concrete voice words. Not "modern" or "professional" — real personality words.
- Formal or casual? Playful or serious? Bold or understated?
- Is there a brand guide, logo, fonts, or colors already? (Get the assets.)
- Show me a site whose vibe you love, and one you hate. Why?
- Is this a brand surface (marketing, POV-driven) or a product surface (task-driven)? (See design.md register.)
- Anything off-limits — words, claims, imagery, competitors you must not resemble?

Dig deeper: reject reflex answers. If they say "clean and modern," ask what that means for them specifically and get examples. Tie voice words back to the audience from Phase 3.

Quality gate:

- [ ] Three specific voice words.
- [ ] Register decided (brand vs product).
- [ ] Existing brand assets collected or confirmed not to exist.
- [ ] At least one reference site loved and one disliked, with reasons.

### Phase 7 — Proof & assets

**Objective:** Gather the evidence and raw materials copy and design will be built from, plus any messaging constraints.

Question bank:

- What proof do you have? (Testimonials, reviews, case studies, metrics, logos, certifications, press.)
- Can you share named results? Real numbers and named clients beat anonymous praise.
- What photography, video, or graphics exist? What is missing?
- Any claims you legally cannot make, or words/imagery you must avoid?
- SEO: known target keywords or topics the copy should speak to?

Dig deeper: proof is where most projects are weak. If proof is thin, say so directly and assign collecting it as a task before copywriting. Confirm there are enough real assets to design with; flag gaps as production tasks.

Quality gate:

- [ ] Proof inventory captured (and gaps flagged as tasks).
- [ ] Asset inventory captured (and gaps flagged as tasks).
- [ ] Messaging constraints (legal claims, banned words) and target keywords noted.

## Branching

Adapt the interview to the project:

- **Lead-gen / services** → go deep on offer, objections, and the single conversion action. Proof and trust are decisive.
- **Ecommerce** → product catalog, price/positioning, shipping/returns, reviews, and the buying journey matter most.
- **Brochure / local business** → keep it tight; prioritize trust, location/contact, and one clear next step. Do not over-scope.
- **Personal brand / portfolio** → voice, POV, and the body of work lead; proof is the work itself.
- **B2B** → multiple stakeholders, longer cycle, comparison and ROI content; capture the buying committee.
- **B2C** → emotion, transformation, and speed to value; reduce friction to the action.

## Synthesis & brief

After Phase 7:

1. Present the complete `discovery/brief.md`.
2. Give your honest assessment as the marketer/salesman: the strongest angle, the biggest risk, and the one thing that will most determine success.
3. List open questions and research tasks (anything marked unknown).
4. Recommend the positioning angle and the primary message the site should lead with.
5. Ask for explicit approval. Revise until approved.
6. On approval, state that discovery is complete and the project is ready for step 2 (Copywriting), which should load [copywriting.md](copywriting.md).

## Quality gates (whole process)

Discovery is not complete until:

- [ ] All 7 phase gates pass (or gaps are explicitly accepted and noted).
- [ ] `discovery/brief.md` is filled in and marked complete.
- [ ] The primary audience, the differentiator, and the primary outcome are unambiguous.
- [ ] Proof and asset gaps are captured as tasks.
- [ ] The user has explicitly approved the brief.
